So what are archives?
Archives are collections of items, of documents, of records which have been selected for permanent
preservation because either they have a value as an evidence or we consider them today as
a future source for historical or other research.
Now records are created by activities of organizations and people, for example by the
public administration.
They serve an active purpose with the current use and some of them are later selected and
preserved as part of an archival collection.
So we don't keep everything that is produced in the public administration but we do a selection
and then take some of the records that are produced and decide to preserve them over
the long run.
Archive collections are usually unique, that's where we have a difference to libraries.
In libraries you have books which have been printed many times and that's also reason,
this uniqueness is a reason why it is so important to take proper care of the archives.
In these archival collections they are kept in specialized institutions which we also call
archives.
Example of this includes national or continental archives.
Here on the right you see a picture of the Swiss Federal Archives in Bern.
On the local level we have city archives for example and then we also have specialized
archives for certain topics like for example the Swiss Economic Archive in Basel or the
archives of contemporary history in Zurich.
Other archival collections are kept by institutions like museums or manuscript departments of large
libraries.
Now the role of archives in our society.
Archives allow a time delayed traceability and monitoring of activities of administration
and governments.
In this way they have an important control function in a democracy.
Of course this control function is not immediate but is time delayed 30 years, 50 years later
we can ask questions as historians of what governments and administrations have done at that time.
Archives provide an evidence of activities which occurred in the past.
They tell stories, document people, identify and are valuable sources of information for
historic research.
They are our recorded memory.
They form an important part of our community, cultural, official and unofficial history.
And last but not least they guarantee legal reliability over the long run.
Now something that is important to keep in mind is the difference between a work and
a record.
And here we can make a rough distinction between libraries and archives.
Because libraries they normally contain works, archives they contain records.
So what is a work in the sense of copyright law?
It's an independent artistic creation such as works of art, literature, music, paintings
and so on.
A work is protected or protected or a protected or a protected creation within the meaning
of the copyright law.
On the other side records they are the output of administrative processes in public administration
but also in private companies in the form of documents or files.
Records are kept in records management systems.
That might be something like a database.
That might be a web server.
That might be other systems, file system and so on and so forth.
Records make activities and decision making processes traceable.
Economic activities, administrative activities and so on.
And what is important, the records that come out of public administration, they are not
a work in the sense of the copyright law.
So we have a difference between these two words.
Now if we have an archival collection, what are the main principles for structuring these
collections?
The first important principle is called the principle of provenance.
Provenance refers to an individual, a family or an organization that created or received
the item in a collection.
The principle of provenance is the respective form that dictates the records or different
origins be kept separate to preserve the context.
So we don't mix up archival content, for example along subjects or geographical provenance
and so on.
We keep archival collections separate from each other according to the context in which
they have been created.
Now if we have a structure in the archive, a first structure along the provenance, then
the next question is how do we structure within the provenance?
This second principle we call the original order.
So archives are kept in the order in which they were originally created or used.
So we don't recreate the complete structure within a collection but we try to reflect
the way in which a collection has been created.
This is essential to understand this when working with archives as a historian so that
this original order is preserved.
First it's important to respect this order as an archivist when you create structures
but then it's important as a historian if you work with archival collections to know
this second principle and to faster find what you're looking for.
This original order allows custodians to protect the authenticity of the records and provides
essential information as to how they were created, kept and used.
And now what are the metadata standards to describe and contextualize archival material?
This is the status quo in archives.
It is about to change to new standards but today we use a standard called ISAG, the International
Standard on Archival Description.
That's the metadata standard for describing the content of archives.
Then we have two other standards, ISRCPF, to describe corporate bodies, persons and families.
That's the agents, they work for example in public administration and then we have a description,
a third standard, a description of processes of functions of business processes, how have
the records been created.
That's the standard ISDF.
Most of the archives today they only use ISAG and rarely ISRCPF or ISDF.
So this is the important standard when you as a historian look for content in an archive.

Now we come to the next topic and that's the goals of digital preservation.
When I talk about digital preservation what is the goal of it, keeping digital information
usable in the long term.
You know all administrations work with digital systems, with records management systems,
with databases and so on and the task, the large task of the public archive is now to
preserve this digital information over the long term.
So what does long term mean in the word of digital archiving across multiple generations
of hardware operating systems, software applications and file formats.
The second term that is important to understand what does usable mean, it means it's identifiable,
findable, we can display it, interpret it and finally reuse it for a new business case
for example or for research.
So the digital archive documents the context of origin of records, ensures the long term
traceability of administrative business processes in the digital world.
The basic challenge of digital preservation is that we have digital information, we pack
it, we send it into the future many years pass by and finally after 40-50 years a historian
unpacks this information, wants to understand, render it, use it and make use of it.
And the challenge is that we have no direct link between a data producer on the left and
an archive user on the right.
So the user cannot go back to the producer and ask about the context, about the system
and so on but we need additional information when we package information to send it into
the future so that an archive user will be able to understand the digital content correctly.
The big question is now what additional information metadata is needed to ensure that package
information remains usable.
And this I want to show you in the following five steps.
Digital archiving always starts in an organization, it's not a guerrilla project but it is a
well-planned task within public archives, within the specialized archives.
So we need money, we need expertise, we need people who can plan, who can install the systems,
maintain the systems and so on.
The first thing is we do not send everything that is produced in an administration into
the future.
We have a strong selection process.
We already had these processes in the paper world and this goes on in the digital world.
So the first question is why should we archive something?
It depends from the legal mandate, from the archival strategy, from the legal basis, are
we allowed to archive it?
And it depends from the historic value.
The second question is what should be archived?
And here we do an evaluation and selection of documents and metadata so we ask what are
the tasks that an organization has?
What is this task?
Something that is important?
Is it something that is on a very low level and it doesn't make sense to preserve it?
It only costs but nobody will use it.
Then we decide not to keep it.
But then the third question is for whom should the archives be preserved?
And often we ask ourselves what is the designated community, the community of stakeholders and
users that the archive serves?
If you don't know for who we are going to archive then it doesn't make sense to keep
the material.
And one thing that is important to keep in mind is that only 3 to 5% of the records and
data produced in the public administration will be preserved in an archive.
And that's not new, that hasn't changed with digital transformation in administrations.
Already in paper based administrations we didn't keep more than that.
This might be shocking for historians who often think that archives they keep everything
that is produced but the archives would be completely overwhelmed if they would try to
keep a much higher percentage of material.
The second step is the preparation of data for archiving.
Once we have decided what we want to keep and the material arrives at the archives the first
thing that is normally done is a virus check, quality control and validation against file
format specifications.
Often we do a file migration to archive formats.
That depends from the archive strategy.
In public archives that take material from administrations which is normally in office
or mail formats.
This is an easy task to do.
If you deal with other kind of data for example with research data normally you keep the original
format and then it's a task of the user to access the archive material with tools that
re-engineer for example the data.
Then we do an extraction of technical and descriptive metadata from the files.
We try to get the necessary metadata for preservation out of the files or we add them automatically
or in the worst case manually to ensure that we have all these metadata that is necessary
to send the package or the file or the database into the future.
We assign unique identifiers to each file.
There are different practices to do that, different systems to do that, they are well
established.
Then we store the files and their metadata in a standardized form in repositories.
So we have a lot of metadata standards.
We don't invent the wheel every time we store something.
We always use standardized metadata formats.
The standardization makes things easier in the future.
In reading rooms for example you only need to deal with very few metadata standards.
You always know what the base of the package will be.
We update the archive catalog with the descriptive metadata and the important thing is that we
document all the steps.
Documentation is an integral part of the archiving process and it ensures traceability
of what has been done to protect the archive.
Next step is the archival storage.
I want to say something about archival storage on two layers.
First we have the application layer, the management of the storage.
We also can call it a repository.
A repository is a piece of software to manage fixed digital information that isn't changed
or isn't changed very often.
Today software has interfaces for receiving and delivering data to the outside world.
These repositories are able to make automated integrity checks.
They can monitor the storage and alert in case of errors.
One important thing is repository systems.
They have user management so only those people who are allowed to access the digital information,
the archive collection, they get the access to do it.
On the lower level below the repository as an application we have what we call the bitstream
preservation.
So the raw storage, storage systems, disk tapes, cloud applications and so on.
On this level we do everything to protect the bitstream through redundancy and diversity.
So we use different storage technology, different vendors, different protocols just to make sure
if one vendor goes out of business we still have copies on systems that they will maintain.
The general requirements for archives, for long-term archives is to have three copies
of the archive collection in two geographical locations and at least one of these copies
is completely offline, for example on tape.
If we have a ransomware attack or something else then we know that at least one copy is
offline and well preserved.
An important thing is to ensure the integrity of the storage to make sure that the storage
is well preserved.
We do that through checksums with the help of checksums.
We also monitor not only on the repository level but normally also additionally on the
storage level the integrity of the storage and repair if necessary.
Now the preservation planning is the function for monitoring changes that may impact the
digital material in the future.
This function distinguishes digital archives from just making backups of the material on
additional storage servers.
So the preservation planning is a proactive activity within the archive where we do technology
watch, so how do the file formats change, how do we have file formats in our repository
that become obsolete, do we have storage technology where the vendor wants to go out of business
or that become obsolete, where there are our tools and infrastructure environments that
we have, do we have to adapt tool, maintain tool, invest in new tools and what are the
access mechanisms for the users, do we have the right tools there.
The next or the second field of preservation planning considers designated communities,
so on the one hand the needs and expectations of our archival users and on the other hand
the needs and expectations and development on the producer side.
So do the producers introduce new systems, new tools, new data models and do we have to
adapt our archival processes based on these changes.
And also with the designated communities, the designated communities does not only refer
to human beings and organization but also to machines.
So what are new emerging tools for example for machine to machine access.
And the last point, the last step in the archival process is the access, the reuse of
our cache, so opening the repository for the outside world for our users, we have to understand
the designated community of users or let's say the designated communities, normally it's
more than one community that we serve for, we serve for the broad public for example
but also for experts like historians who want to dive very deep into the data and use raw
data and not only copies for the web for example.
We have to map the archival formats, often we have things like TIFF, we have made OCR

Sometimes we also have contradiction between efficient access and data protection or lockup

So as archives we want to give access, basically give access to everything but we are restricted

And we have to deal with this contradiction in as pragmatic way as possible.
