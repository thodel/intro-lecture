In this short introductory lecture, I will talk about digitalization from a gender studies
perspective, more precisely from a social science perspective on gender and technology.
I will thus first sketch very briefly what it means analytically to think about gender
and technology as being co-constructed and how such a constructivist perspective can
inform our understanding of digitalization.
I will then raise three issues that are at the center of public discussion and research
on digitalization and gender, namely the digitalization of work and digitalization
as an era of work itself, second digital technologies as gendered technical artifacts, and third
digital media technologies as arenas for gender politics.
As I can only scratch upon the surface of this topic, I have inserted texts for further
reading and where possible also links to online sources, so as for you to individually
dig deeper into topics of your interest.
To conclude this lecture, I will shortly revisit some lessons learned and consider the question
of how to move on from here, that is how to shape the digital transformation of society.
As mentioned before, my take on digitalization is informed by social science perspectives
on gender and technology being co-constructed.
What does that mean for how to think analytically about digitalization?
First of all, a constructivist perspective on gender and technology rejects any essentialist
or naturalist thinking, but instead focuses on the social practices, cultural norms, and
societal structures that only just bring about gender as well as technology.
Put differently, neither gender nor technology are thought of as just given, but they are
understood as social constructs, as the cumulative effects of what happens and is done within
society.
Such a constructivist perspective, hence, directs our attention to precisely what is
done by members of society in order to bring about gender and technology as we then come
to encounter them.
From this analytical stance to gender and technology also follows that as they are not
given in any way, the form they are taking could be different.
The gender order needn't be patriarchal and heteronormative.
Women need not be more likely to be in danger of poverty due to working in poorly paid occupations,
and trans and non-binary persons needn't be excluded due to a gender order that knows
only two genders.
Similarly, technology needn't thrive on the exploitation of natural resources nor need
digitalization be primarily used, quote, in the service of profit, for a few, surveillance
of the minoritized and efficiency amidst scarcity, as Catherine DiNazio and Lauren Klein criticized
so pointedly in their book, Data Feminism.
In feminist technology studies, these perspectives are joined to consider gender and technology
not only as constructed, but as co-constructed.
This means to look at gender and technology as inextricably interwoven as shaping one
another with technology being a resource for the construction of gender, while gender
is at the same time understood as a resource for the construction of technology.
Again, what does it mean more precisely and how can we apply this perspective when thinking
about digitalization?
First, this means to analytically distinguish three levels on which digitalization as technical
development and gender are being co-constructed.
A cultural level of knowledge that comprises not only technical know-how in a more narrow
sense, but also knowledge about digitalization and the ways we as a society think about digitalization
as well as how we think about gender, what gender norms we adhere to, which ideas of
femininity and masculinity are dominant.
When it comes to digitalization, for instance, one important insight is that technological
development is not happening in and out of itself, like techno-deterministic narratives
suggest.
Rather, it's promoted and driven by mostly techno-optimistic discourses, politics and
stakeholders.
When we look at digitalization, it's especially the promise of solving all kinds of problems
from poverty to climate change that drives and legitimizes the call for developing and
deploying ever more digital technologies in almost every sphere of society.
This techno-solutionism, as Afghani Morozov calls it, thereby draws heavily on masculine
ideas about technological control and power and the supposed neutrality of machines being
superior to the bodily situated knowledge and practices of people, as I've analyzed in
an article in the Promises of Big Data.
Second, digitalization as a label stands for the development and deployment of a highly
heterogeneous amount of technologies and technical artifacts ranging from social media
sites, speech assistance, smart home devices and robots to algorithmic decision-making
systems, body scanners, self-service units in supermarkets or banks, to name just a few.
All of these technical artifacts are designed and shaped by specific people in specific
development contexts with specific ideas in mind, thus influencing the very form and
shape that these technologies take.
Excuse me for a second.
These development contexts are gendered in so far as they are sites of gender work relations
with big tech companies being dominated by mostly white middle class men situated in a
global north, bringing gender norms to the table and also to developing digital artifacts.
The concept of script and technology studies grasps this idea by pointing out that designers
and engineers as Madeline Aklesh writes, construct many different representations of the users
and use context and objectify these representations in technological choices, effectively inscribing
their ideas about users and scenarios of use into the final artifact by grounding their
design decisions on these very ideas.
As gender research on technology has shown, these scripts rarely stem from thorough research
but are mostly derived from personal experience.
This is why feminist scholars on technology speak of a so-called eye methodology being
prevalent amongst engineers and designers.
Thereby, those responsible for designing, constructing and shaping technical artifacts
imagine themselves as ideal users, thus founding their design choices on their own preferences,
skillsets and wishes.
This is of course especially problematic in a society in which those in control of developing
and shaping technologies constitute a rather small and homogeneous group of people, thus
not being representative of the people who end up using the technologies.
As a consequence, many technologies reflect the perspective of those designing them, excluding
or hindering the use for those not being presented, such as Google Maps calculating the shortest
path without taking into account that steps, for instance, are impossible to overcome for
a person in a wheelchair or a woman pushing a prem, or considering that touch screens
need or expect fine motor skills often no longer available to elderly people.
When designers and engineers bring, I'll buy it pre-refractively and thus unintentionally.
Gendered ideas to the table, they engender technical artifacts in a very specific way,
thus objectifying these ideas and making them durable.
As we have all come to live with digital assistants that mirror the stereotypical image of female
assistants to the extent that Alexa or Siri have been shown to keep a calm, polite and
always serving attitude even when literally being attacked in a sexist way.
At the same time, and I now want to turn to the third level of practice, acting and doing,
handling technical artifacts and acting with digital technologies may take quite different
forms than initially intended. That is, the level of knowledge must not be and often is not
identical to the level of actual practice. Think, for instance, about the introduction of
industrial robots and the consequences it might have for the workforce.
Introduced as making production faster, more efficient and less prone to mistakes,
it will likely have consequences for the gender relations among the workforce.
For those who are let off due to rationalization and cutting down on personnel,
this development might add to a crisis of working class masculinity that is strongly
linked to employment and earning a life for one's family. For those who stay on,
not only the concrete work practices change, but with it also the conditions for doing gender,
for doing working class masculinity. It's now no longer centered around the idea of heavy,
bodily labor, but around the image of the technically skilled machine operator.
From these examples, we can thus see how digitalization becomes a resource for the
construction of gender in a multitude of ways and vice versa. To summarize so far,
thinking of gender and digitalization as co-constructed means to reject any
essentialist thinking about gender as well as technological development. But to look into how
specific gender and technology relations shape one another in specific contexts,
especially taking into account the different forms of shaping, different forms of becoming
together are possible. And thus to acknowledge that other technologies are just as possible as
our other gender relations and norms. Let me now turn to the first of the three issues I want to
address in today's introductory lecture. How is digitalization changing gendered work relations?
As the example of the introduction of industrial robots and ensuing consequences for working
class men and masculinity has already shown, there are no generalizable statements possible when
we look at the digitalization of work. Because work settings, gendered work relations, and the
technologies introduced differ so strongly, it's instead necessary to conduct thorough in-depth
analysis of specific work settings and occupations. Asking how is the introduction of a specific
technology affecting the gendered structure of the workforce? How is it renegotiating the
distribution of labor, for instance, amongst men and women? Whose skills and competences are valued
or devalued? Whose perspectives, preferences, skills are favored? Whose are being marginalized?
And although overall assessments are not possible, I want to draw your attention to one
insight from feminist perspectives on work and digitalization. Feminist perspectives on work
and digitalization draw our attention to the often unseen, unacknowledged and ignored aspects
of work. Such as the work of content moderators or dataminers, that is, all by being essential to
digitalization, often done as unqualified, poorly paid, hardly appreciated click work
by an anonymous workforce that is mostly located in the so-called global south.
And is thus standing in strong contrast to the almost glamorous, highly visible and well-paid
work of so-called big tech workers in the global north, be it data scientists, AI scholars, or
software engineers. Going a step further, Kylie Jarrett has coined the term digital housewife
to direct our view to the similarities of housework that is still mostly done by women
and all the invisible, unpaid and unappreciated work we all do when using digital technologies,
thus adding indispensable worth to digital companies such as posting a comment on social media sites,
which we rarely even acknowledge as work, let alone for what we expect to be paid,
but without which the respective social media companies would be worthless and their product
just wouldn't exist. When it comes to digital technologies, as specific technical artifacts,
feminist technology studies draws our attention to the gender norms inscribed into digital
technologies, gendering them fundamentally and at the same time having gendered effects
for the people who use them and or are put to use through them. Again, the question of what
gender norms are being objectified in digital technologies can only be answered with regards
to concrete artifacts, with some gendering being more obvious and others being more subtle
or implicit, at least for the majority of us. I want to hint at one example
for how social gender norms and a deeply heteronormative gender order takes the existence
of only two genders as the norm are inscribed into the construction of digital technologies.
Sasha Konstantzichak has demonstrated how security scanners at airports are continuously
flagging trans women and men as security risks, because their bodies do not conform to neither
of the two gender body norms the machine has been programmed to match the person to.
They do neither conform to the supposed norm of a man's nor of a woman's body. Being flagged
then results in the trans person being scanned manually, raising the question of who is appropriate
to scan them. A man or woman security officer highlighting again they're not fitting into the
binary gender order and potentially adding another traumatizing event to their personal history.
This example shows how social gender norms are inscribed into technical artifacts that are then
taking part in constructing gender masculinity and femininity. By, as in this case, deciding about
the right and wrong gendered bodies, literally signaling which genders are to be acknowledged
as true or false and which are to be put into question.
A third topic that is of importance when talking about digitalization and gender
are digital media, especially social media, and how they are used to promote gender politics.
Ever since the early days of the internet, feminist scholars have pondered upon the question
whether a virtual cyberspace would path the way for freeing us from our bodily gender,
allowing for people to play with or even swap their gender identities as Sherry Turkle elaborated
on with regards to digital arbiters. Nowadays, interest has shifted to social media sites as
arenas for negotiating gender politics. Assessments of if and how digital media sites
promote a feminist agenda or are on the contrary sites for anti-feminist engagement
are so far very ambivalent, nearly twofolded. On the one hand, hate speech and sexual violence
directed at women and LGBTQI plus person demonstrate that the internet and social
media sites are no gender neutral spheres, but are just as permediate by heteronormative
patriarchal norms as is the so-called offline world. On the other hand, however,
digital media offer a platform for raising the issue of sexual violence and discrimination
on the basis of gender and sexuality, thus demonstrating that gender discrimination,
for instance, is no singular event, but a structural component of how our society is made up in works.
The so-called hashtag feminism with, for instance, hashtag MeToo that was initially started by black
women having literally gone viral globally can thus unfold very empowering effects
by allowing for those otherwise minoritized and silenced to join their forces to speak out together,
crossing even geographical boundaries and to counter strategies of shaming the victims
or privatizing the problem. As lawsuits and new regulations against sexual violence and
gender discrimination show, raising awareness to a wider public may eventually lead to changes in
other areas of society, making digital media technologies important arenas for feminist
net politics. What can we learn from this multitude of heterogeneous insights into the
interrelations of gender and digitalization? Most importantly, what can we learn for how to engage
with the digital transformation of society? As you might expect, there is again no one answer
to these questions. I therefore want to draw your attention at some last takeaway messages.
As the examples presented have hopefully demonstrated, digitalization is indeed a very
complex, highly heterogeneous ongoing and thus open-ended socio-technical process
that is at different levels and in different ways shaped by and is shaping gender relations,
norms and practices. Taken seriously, this insight means that there simply cannot be
the one answer to any question concerning the digital transformation of society.
Instead, we are called upon to put in the effort and to look very closely at and study the multitude
ways and forms that digitalization is interrelated with gender, be it to ask how the introduction of
a specific technology changes the gendered work relations in a concrete occupational setting,
be it to scrutinize the explicit and implicit gender norms inscribed in digital artifacts
or to differentiate the articulations of gender politics in digital media.
As a deeply social process, digitalization and the way it's interrelated with gender can be shaped.
Again, taking this insight seriously, we can at least theoretically all take part in shaping the
digital transformation of society. Following Donner-Haraway, who has for many decades advocated
for taking responsibility for and engaging in technoscientific developments, this offers the
chance of developing technologies that allow to build a world that is less structured and
organized by power asymmetries and inequalities. In order to do so, she and many others who have
thought about how to intervene in technoscientific development call for participatory design
approaches. Participatory design is guided by the idea that technological development should
start from the everyday practices, wishes and needs of the later users.
Thereby, it's especially those who speak from the margins of society, such as the elderly,
the less affluent, the disabled, trans and non-binary people that promise to offer perspectives
for technological development that not so much reproduce existing inequalities,
such as gender inequalities, but that challenge them or even contribute to reducing them.
With this slightly optimistic note that is founded on a cyber feminist idea,
instead of techno-solutionist ones, I want to end this short lecture.
