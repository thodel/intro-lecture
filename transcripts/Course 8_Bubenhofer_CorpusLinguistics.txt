There's also another interesting theoretical background.
I like to talk about corpus pragmatics for the kind of corpus linguistics that interests
me.
Pragmatics is, after all, an important basic assumption of linguistics which can be grounded
in Austin and Sears Speech Act theory.
Speaking is not simply describing something or conveying information, but is acting.
This idea has prevailed in many disciplines to this day and has also inspired the so-called
linguistic turn.
Corpus pragmatics builds on this and sees traces of linguistic action in patterns on
the linguistic surface.
Thus it becomes clear that social action, social discourses become visible on the linguistic
surface.
Now it's interesting that quantitative methods are strong in identifying linguistic patterns.
This is what corpus linguistics can do.
The identified patterns can now be interpreted in different ways.
They are expressions of grammar, linguistic variation, etc.
But they are also related to social and cultural phenomena.
They are symptoms of this phenomena and at the same time they constitute them.
We can see this in the example of greetings.
In spoken language we find many linguistic patterns of greetings used the same or similar
formulas again and again.
This is due to the fact that greetings play an important social role in interaction.
So corpus pragmatics is about finding clues about how society and culture works and how
it changes by analyzing language use.
Clearly, corpus linguistics is an empirical method.
Thus unlike rationalism it is concerned with finding insights through observation.

There are many different empirical methods.

Now we have already talked a lot about corpora but we have not yet defined more precisely
what a corpus is.
Here is a definition by Lemnitzer and Zinsmeister.
Corpus is a collection of written or spoken utterances.
The data of the corpus is typically digitized, that means stored on a computer and machine
readable.
The components of the corpus, the texts consist of the data itself and possible metadata describing
this data and from linguistic annotations associated with this data.
The definition makes clear that of course corpora existed before the invention of the
computer but digitization has drastically changed the ways in which corpora can be analyzed.
Metadata is any data that describes a text in more detail, for example author, source,
date etc.
By linguistic annotations we mean for example part of speech, to each word is added, usually
automatically the corresponding part of speech.
Often the base form, the infinitive is also added, the syntactic function or similar information.
So what is corpus linguistics?
Here again a definition by Lemnitzer and Zinsmeister.
Corpus linguistics refers to the description of utterances, natural languages of its elements
and structures and theory building based on it.
Based on analysis of authentic texts collected in corpora.
So it is about authentic texts, in other words texts that have not been created especially
for the analysis but that have been created for other reasons.
And what we use afterwards to analyze them with corpus linguistic methods.
